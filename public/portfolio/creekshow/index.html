The Creek Show highlights how public art can convey messages and impact communities.

Alex Contreras

Students at the University of Texas at Austin created an installation for Waterloo Greenway’s Creek Show. Creek Show is an annual light-based art show in Austin that began in 2014. It aims to bring awareness to the restoration of Waller Creek and the parks soon to be built around it. This year, during its run of 11 days, it attracted more than 60,000 people.

Homer Gonzales, communications coordinator for Waterloo Greenway, said, ”Creek Show is such a great way to invite people to discover an area of downtown Austin they might not have ever experienced before. It's also a good opportunity to educate the public about our project and mission at Waterloo Greenway.”

After seeing one of their previous installations, Creek Show approached Texas Applied Arts to ask for a submission. Texas Applied Arts brings together students from many disciplines such as art, theatre, and engineering to provide the opportunity for practical design experience. While working on the project, students had to abide by a timeline, collaborate, and fulfill a design brief with real-life consequences. The idea for the Creek Monster Habitat was derived from the mascot of the Creek Show, the Creek Monster.

Habitat

Karen Maness, instructor for the Creek Monster Habitat class, said, “I'm really excited about the idea of creating classes around projects that have real time deadlines with some real-world ramifications if they don't actually meet the deadline.”

The Habitat invites you into the Monster’s home. The Monster can be imagined as a sort of guardian for the Creek that lies within an urban environment. Texas Applied Arts wanted to portray nature as an alluring source of healing. The Habitat draws attention to the importance of caring for the Earth without words.

Public Art

Art can be a vessel for starting conversations and getting people to slow down and reflect. Impactful pieces keep their core message in mind and draw attention to it, often by drawing out strong emotional reactions.

”I feel like art is a gateway to advocate for almost anything. I mean, it’s a vehicle and it can be powerfully succinct in expressing what words cannot,” said Maness.

The scale of the installations in Creek Show allow one to immerse themselves in the art and take in the message. The way Creek Show integrates art into the Creek allows a familiar space to be transformed and viewed from a different perspective.

Kevin Sullivan of the meander team said, “It’s been really fun seeing and encouraging people to come, not only experience Waller Creek which they’ve probably walked by many times, but come see Creek Show and kind of come take a minute to appreciate public art.”

Creek Show is an open call so people from all backgrounds can send in ideas. Many of the selected submissions this year had experience in design or architecture, but never necessarily thought of themselves as artists and don’t make art for a living.

Sunny Schneberger of the Light House team said, “I think we’re all sort of reveling in the fact that for two weeks we get to be called artists instead of our other titles.”

Public art can become part of a city’s culture. For example, the iconic and often- Instagrammed murals around Austin.

James Cornetet of the String Theory team said, “You can be a non-trained artist, slap up a mural on the wall, and it can have a profound impact on an entire town. And we’ve seen that over and over, and I think that’s what’s special about [Austin]."

Because of its ability to solidify itself within a location’s identity, public art can be a way to give back to the community. Putting time and energy into a place is a way to connect with it.

”[Creek Show has] been a really great experience to give back to the community that I feel like has given me so much,” said Sullivan.

Everyone should have the opportunity to create and contribute to their community through art. As with anything else, avoiding gate-keeping allows unique perspectives to be expressed. Things like Creek Show give opportunities to those who aren’t traditionally “serious artists.”

“If you look at artists, and I won't name them, but there's a lot of great artists in this town. I'm not sure that they would be given the same shake in another town because it's not serious art or it doesn't fit that narrow definition,” said Cornetet.

Everyone can contribute to a piece of art, regardless of skill set. In the class for the Creek Monster Habitat, students come from all across campus, including engineering. Many of the installations had to get help from someone with experience in construction in order to bring their art to life.

Mitch Flora of the Aurora team said, “I think [getting involved in art is] just having a really good story and knowing how to connect that to people and other skill sets you need.”

Creek Show has been able to bring together people from all walks of life to create art. Art has given them the chance to voice their messages and give back to the community of Austin. Art is able to connect people together and weave itself into a city’s culture. The positive impact that Creek Show has not only on Waterloo Greenway’s mission, but the city of Austin as a whole, will hopefully encourage more support for public art.
